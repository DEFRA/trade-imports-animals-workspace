# Escalation and Ambiguity Resolution Patterns

This file covers when an agent should hand off to a human and how it should resolve ambiguous inputs rather than guess. Consult it when designing system prompts, tool-result handling, or escalation policies for any customer-facing or decision-making agent — especially when deciding what counts as a hand-off trigger versus a case the agent should still attempt.

---

## 1. Escalate on explicit request, policy gaps, or lack of progress — not on "this feels hard"

There are three appropriate escalation triggers: the customer explicitly asks for a human, the situation falls into a policy exception or gap (not merely a complex case), or the agent cannot make meaningful progress.

Anything outside these three is not a valid trigger. "The case is complex" or "the tooling required multiple steps" is not, on its own, a reason to escalate — agents are expected to handle complexity within their capability envelope.

Encode the three triggers explicitly in the system prompt and reinforce them with few-shot examples showing both an escalate case and a resolve case for similar-looking inputs.

---

## 2. Honour explicit human-agent requests immediately — do not investigate first

When a customer demands a human, route them to one. Do not first attempt to diagnose the problem, ask qualifying questions, or try a tool call "just in case it's quick".

The asymmetry matters: an unwanted investigation step on top of an explicit hand-off request compounds the customer's frustration and signals that the agent is overriding their stated preference.

```text
Right — customer says "I want to speak to a person":
  → Route to human immediately. No diagnostic questions.

Wrong:
  → "I can help with that. First, can you tell me the order number?"
```

Reserve diagnostic flow for cases where the request is for resolution, not for a human.

---

## 3. For straightforward issues, acknowledge and offer to resolve — escalate only on reiteration

When the issue is within the agent's capability, the right pattern is to acknowledge the customer's frustration, offer to resolve it directly, and only escalate if they reiterate that they want a human.

This balances two failure modes: escalating too eagerly on every frustrated message (wasting human capacity, slowing the customer) and stonewalling a clear hand-off request (frustrating the customer further). Acknowledgement + offer + escalate-on-reiterate threads between them.

Contrast this with Rule 2: an *explicit demand* for a human is immediate hand-off. A *frustrated message about the issue* is acknowledge-and-offer, with escalation if they push back.

---

## 4. Do not use sentiment or self-reported confidence as escalation proxies

Sentiment-based escalation (escalate because the customer sounds upset) and self-reported confidence scores (escalate because the model says it is unsure) are unreliable signals for actual case complexity.

Sentiment correlates poorly with whether the agent can resolve the issue — a calm customer can have a policy-exception case, and a frustrated customer can have a one-click refund. Self-reported confidence drifts and is gameable by prompt phrasing; it does not reflect the structural reasons the case is or is not within scope.

Escalation policy must be anchored to the structural triggers in Rule 1 (explicit request, policy gap, no progress), not to model-internal or tone-derived heuristics.

---

## 5. Escalate when policy is ambiguous or silent on the customer's specific request

If the policy addresses a related case but not the customer's actual request, that is a policy gap — escalate. Do not extrapolate from adjacent policy to fill the silence.

The canonical example is competitor price matching when the policy only addresses own-site price adjustments. The agent should not invent a rule by analogy; it should escalate to a human who can either apply discretion or update the policy.

```text
Policy: "If our own price drops within 14 days, we refund the difference."

Customer: "A competitor is selling this £20 cheaper. Will you match?"

Wrong: invent a competitor-match rule by analogy and apply it.
Right: identify the policy gap, escalate.
```

The same applies whenever the policy is silent: silence is not implicit permission, and it is not implicit denial — it is a gap that needs human judgement.

---

## 6. Ask for additional identifiers when tool results return multiple matches

When a lookup tool returns more than one candidate match for the customer, the agent must ask for an additional identifier (order number, postcode, email, account ID) rather than picking one using heuristics.

Heuristic selection — "most recent", "highest value", "alphabetically first" — silently routes the conversation to the wrong record. The customer cannot see the disambiguation has happened, and downstream actions (refunds, address changes) land on the wrong account.

```text
Tool result: 3 customers match "John Smith"

Wrong:
  → Pick the most recent active account and proceed.

Right:
  → "I have several matches for that name. Could you confirm
     your order number or the postcode on the account?"
```

Make this an explicit instruction in the system prompt, ideally with a few-shot example that demonstrates the disambiguation question instead of a silent pick.
