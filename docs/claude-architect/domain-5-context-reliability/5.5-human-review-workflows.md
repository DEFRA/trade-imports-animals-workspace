# Human Review Workflows and Confidence Calibration

This file covers how to design human-in-the-loop review for extraction and classification pipelines: where to spend limited reviewer attention, how to calibrate model confidence against labelled data, and how to detect regressions that aggregate metrics will hide. Consult it when you are deciding which extractions go to humans, when you are tempted to quote a single headline accuracy number, or when you are planning to reduce human review on a previously-supervised workflow.

---

## 1. Do not trust aggregate accuracy — segment by document type and field

A "97% overall" accuracy figure masks the segments where the model fails hardest. A pipeline can hit 97% in aggregate while extracting one field on one document type at 60% accuracy, and that single bad segment is usually the one that hurts in production.

Before reducing human review on any workflow, break accuracy down by document type and by field. Verify performance is consistent across every segment that matters, not just on average. A segment that underperforms is a signal to keep human review on that segment specifically, not to lower the overall threshold.

---

## 2. Calibrate field-level confidence scores against a labelled validation set

Have the model output confidence scores at the field level, not just for the whole document. Then calibrate those scores using a labelled validation set: the threshold above which an extraction can skip human review must be derived from observed accuracy on real labels, not from the raw model score alone.

A raw "0.95" from the model is not 95% accuracy. The calibration step maps model-reported confidence onto empirical accuracy on held-out labelled data, and that mapping is what routes review attention.

---

## 3. Route low-confidence and ambiguous extractions to human review first

Reviewer capacity is the scarce resource. Spend it where the model is least sure, or where the source documents themselves are ambiguous or contradictory. High-confidence extractions on clean source documents are the cheapest to skip; low-confidence extractions and noisy/conflicting sources are the most expensive to get wrong unattended.

Prioritise the human queue by:

- Low model confidence on the field.
- Ambiguous or contradictory source documents, even if model confidence is high.
- Document types or fields with known weaker validated performance (see rule 1).

---

## 4. Stratified-sample high-confidence extractions for ongoing error measurement

Even after a threshold has been validated, do not stop sampling above it. Run stratified random sampling across the high-confidence band to keep measuring the real error rate and to catch novel error patterns that the original validation set did not contain.

Stratification matters: a uniform random sample of the high-confidence pool will be dominated by the largest document type and the most common fields, so rare segments stop being measured. Stratify by document type and by field so each segment continues to produce a measurable error rate over time.

This is the early-warning system for distribution shift — a new document layout, a new field format, a new source — that would otherwise quietly degrade accuracy until a downstream consumer complains.

---

## 5. Validate by segment before automating, not after

The order is: measure per-document-type and per-field accuracy on labelled data, confirm every segment clears its threshold, then reduce human review. Reversing that order — automating first and watching for complaints — pushes the cost of model failure onto downstream consumers and hides exactly the segments where confidence calibration was wrong.

A segment that has not been validated at its current production volume is not a candidate for reduced review, regardless of how the aggregate looks.
