# Information Provenance and Uncertainty Handling

Covers how to preserve source attribution and surface uncertainty when multiple subagents feed findings into a synthesis step. Consult this file when designing research, document-analysis, or fan-out/synthesis pipelines where the coordinator merges results from several sources and downstream readers need to trust — or challenge — individual claims. The core failure mode it guards against is compression: summarisation steps that drop the claim-to-source mapping, leaving the final report indistinguishable from confabulation.

---

## 1. Require subagents to emit structured claim-source mappings

Every claim a subagent produces must travel with its source — URL, document name, and a relevant excerpt — as structured data, not free prose. Summarisation downstream then has something to preserve and merge, rather than a paragraph it has to re-attribute by guessing.

When the mapping is implicit ("the report says...") the synthesis agent compresses it away on the next pass. When it is structured, the synthesis agent can carry it through verbatim and the final reader can trace any claim back to its origin.

```json
{
  "claim": "Q3 2024 revenue grew 12% year-on-year",
  "source_url": "https://example.com/q3-report.pdf",
  "source_name": "Q3 2024 Investor Report",
  "excerpt": "Total revenue for Q3 2024 was $1.34B, up 12% from Q3 2023."
}
```

Right: subagent returns a list of objects shaped like the above. Wrong: subagent returns a paragraph that mentions the figure without a per-claim attribution block.

---

## 2. Preserve claim-source mappings through every synthesis step

A synthesis agent that receives structured claim-source mappings must merge them, not flatten them. The mapping is the unit of work — combining two findings means combining their sources, not picking one and discarding the other.

If a synthesis prompt instructs the agent to "write a summary of the findings", the structured attribution will be dropped on the first pass. Instruct it instead to produce output that still carries the per-claim source list, and to merge sources when two subagents independently report the same claim.

---

## 3. Annotate conflicts; do not arbitrarily reconcile them

When two credible sources disagree on a statistic, the synthesis output must show both values with their source attribution. Picking one silently — or averaging them — destroys information the downstream reader needs to judge the disagreement.

The coordinator, not the subagent, decides how to reconcile. The subagent's job is to surface the conflict cleanly.

```markdown
Wrong:
  Market size: $4.2B (2024).

Right:
  Market size (2024):
    - $4.2B per Gartner, "Market Forecast 2024" (published 2024-03)
    - $3.8B per IDC, "Worldwide Tracker H1 2024" (published 2024-06)
  Note: methodologies differ — Gartner includes services revenue, IDC does not.
```

---

## 4. Require publication or collection dates on every data point

Two sources that look contradictory are often just measuring different points in time. Without dates in the structured output, the synthesis agent treats temporal differences as factual contradictions and either flags spurious conflicts or papers over real ones.

Make dates a required field in the subagent's output schema — publication date for documents, data collection date for statistics. Missing-date entries should be flagged, not silently passed through.

```json
{
  "claim": "Unemployment rate: 4.1%",
  "source_name": "BLS Monthly Report",
  "publication_date": "2024-11-01",
  "data_collection_period": "2024-10"
}
```

---

## 5. Structure reports to separate well-established from contested findings

The synthesis output should have explicit sections that distinguish findings the sources agree on from findings where they disagree. Burying contested claims in a single narrative tells the reader everything is equally trustworthy.

Preserve original source characterisations and methodological context inside the contested section — the reader needs to know *why* sources disagree, not just *that* they do.

```markdown
## Well-established findings
- Revenue grew year-on-year in 2024 (agreed by sources A, B, C).

## Contested findings
- Total addressable market estimate:
  - Source A: $12B, top-down sizing from analyst panel.
  - Source B: $8B, bottom-up from customer survey.
  The methodologies disagree on what counts as in-scope; see Source B p.14.
```

---

## 6. Carry conflicts forward from document analysis — do not resolve at the leaf

When a subagent doing document analysis sees conflicting values inside a single corpus, it should include both with annotation and let the coordinator decide. Resolving at the leaf hides the conflict from the only agent with cross-document context to adjudicate it.

The synthesis layer has visibility the leaf does not — other documents, the user's question, the coordinator's policy on how to weigh sources. A leaf-level "I picked the newer one" collapses that decision before it can be made well.

---

## 7. Render each content type in its native format

Financial data belongs in tables. News and qualitative findings belong in prose. Technical findings belong in structured lists. Converting everything to a single uniform format — usually prose — loses the structure the original source used to carry meaning.

A table of quarterly revenue prose-ified into "Q1 was $1.1B, Q2 was $1.2B, Q3 was $1.34B..." is harder to scan, harder to verify against the source, and easier for downstream summarisation to mis-quote. Match the rendering to the content type.
